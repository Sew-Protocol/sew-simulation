## Mini glossary (shared)

Scope note: This document is **counterfactual-engine-focused** (node-level evaluator semantics/output keys); for the **broader SPE concept and mechanism-design framing**, start with `docs/subgame-perfect-improvements.md`.

- **SPE proxy**: Bounded, replay-based subgame-perfect-equilibrium evidence; not a formal full-game proof.
- **Decision node**: A strategic action point evaluated for deviation profitability.
- **Proper subgame node**: Node where relevant state/history is publicly checkable for SPE-style evaluation.
- **Information-set node**: Node where private/hidden information affects action availability or payoff inference.
- **Continuation policy**: Rule for downstream behavior after a deviation (e.g., `:trace-following`, `:policy-response`).
- **Utility spec**: Declared payoff model used for comparisons (e.g., terminal-realized or reputation-aware utility).
- **Local regret**: `best-alt-utility - chosen-utility` at a node.
- **Bundle regret**: Bounded multi-step approximation built from local regret.
- **Epsilon thresholds**: Materiality tolerances (`:epsilon-abs`, `:epsilon-rel`) for practical pass/fail semantics.
- **Counterexample**: Structured profitable-deviation evidence record.
- **Off-path coverage**: How many generated/evaluated/inconclusive nodes were explored beyond the realized path.
- **Proof sketch**: Human-readable Claim/Method/Checked/Result summary emitted with SPE observed output.

Essential improvements
1. Explicit continuation-policy modeling

Local regret only becomes meaningful if the alternative branch is evaluated against a clearly defined continuation policy for all later decisions.

Why it matters:

If you replay an alternative action but then let later behavior drift inconsistently, the regret number is not well-defined.
Subgame analysis is really about: “If the agent deviates here, and everyone thereafter follows the specified continuation behavior, was deviation profitable?”

What to add:

A formal continuation-policy contract.
Clear separation between:
the chosen historical action at the node,
the alternative action being tested,
the downstream policy used after that deviation.
Ideally, a mechanism to specify whether downstream actions are:
replayed from trace if still valid,
regenerated by policy,
or recomputed by the equilibrium module.

Without this, regret figures can look precise while being conceptually unstable.

2. Information-set correctness

You need to distinguish between decisions made with full state knowledge and decisions made under partial or role-bounded knowledge.

Why it matters:

A counterfactual action should only be considered if it was actually available given the acting agent’s information at that time.
Otherwise you risk testing “clairvoyant” deviations.

What to add:

A notion of decision context or information set.
At minimum:
acting agent,
observable state,
hidden/unavailable state,
time and ordering context,
available protocol actions from that perspective.
Alternative generation should be constrained by the information set, not just the full simulator state.

This is especially important for disputes, escalation timing, and any case where agents infer state from public events rather than privileged internals.

3. Strong utility semantics

The subgame checker needs a single rigorous definition of utility, including timing, costs, and risk treatment.

Why it matters:

If utility is underspecified, regret becomes misleading.
In your domain, utility is not just “did the agent get more funds?” It likely includes:
asset outcomes,
slashing/bond loss,
fees,
time delay,
capital lock-up,
gas/transaction costs,
maybe reputation or future eligibility in multi-epoch settings.

What to add:

A canonical utility function interface.
Clear choice between:
terminal realized utility,
discounted utility,
risk-adjusted utility,
or protocol-specific payout utility.
Explicit handling of incomparable or undefined outcomes.

This is one of the most important additions because almost every later result depends on it.

4. Subgame-valid replay boundaries

You already noted replay boundary rules, but this is important enough to treat as a first-class design concern.

Why it matters:

If too much is frozen, alternatives become artificial.
If too little is frozen, results become nondeterministic or incomparable.

What to define precisely:

What is frozen:
pre-state snapshot,
block time,
pending events,
available actors,
environmental parameters.
What may vary:
the chosen action at the node,
downstream actions,
resulting state evolution.
Whether same-block ordering is preserved or re-opened.
How exogenous events are treated.

This should probably become a documented semantic contract rather than just an implementation detail.

5. Support for “no decisive verdict” classifications

A mature checker should distinguish between pass/fail and structural inconclusiveness.

Why it matters:

Some nodes won’t admit meaningful alternatives.
Some nodes will have multiple actions with effectively equal utility.
Some nodes may depend on missing continuation semantics.

What to add:

Explicit result classes such as:
pass,
fail,
epsilon-pass,
inconclusive-insufficient-alternatives,
inconclusive-undefined-utility,
inapplicable-node-type.
Aggregate reporting should preserve these distinctions.

This prevents the system from over-claiming equilibrium evidence.

Very high value improvements
6. Multi-step deviation checking

Phase 4 sounds like a one-node deviation checker. That is useful, but many strategic failures come from short sequences, not single moves.

Why it matters:

An agent may tolerate a locally worse immediate move because it improves a later position.
Example patterns:
take a small loss now to trigger escalation later,
avoid a challenge now to preserve bond/capital for another step,
issue a strategic verdict knowing a later response is profitable.

What to add:

Bounded multi-step counterfactuals, e.g. depth-2 or depth-3 deviations.
Possibly “deviation bundles”:
deviate at node N,
and again at next decision for the same actor.
A cost cap or branching cap to control explosion.

This is one of the best second-wave upgrades because it catches strategic patterns that one-step regret misses.

7. Counterfactual response modeling

Once one agent deviates, other agents may respond differently. That should be modeled explicitly.

Why it matters:

Static replay of the original future trace can understate or overstate profitability.
In dispute systems, the whole point is that deviations provoke reactions.

What to add:

Response policies for other agents after a deviation.
At least two modes:
trace-following if valid,
policy-response mode.
Later, support best-response or bounded-response approximations.

This materially improves realism.

8. Alternative-generation quality

A subgame checker is only as good as the alternatives it tests.

Why it matters:

If the alternative set is weak, the system may wrongly conclude “no profitable deviation.”
In adversarial domains, the most valuable action is often not the obvious one.

What to improve:

Domain-specific alternative libraries by node type:
challenge now / later / not at all,
escalate immediately / wait / decline,
settle partial / full / reject,
resolver verdict options,
bond withdrawal timing,
repeated or delayed actions.
Heuristic prioritization of promising alternatives.
Optional adversarial strategy generators.

This is probably the highest-value usability improvement after correctness essentials.

9. Epsilon-equilibrium support

In practice, exact zero-regret is often too strict or noisy.

Why it matters:

Minor numerical differences, gas noise, or tie-like cases can make exact pass/fail brittle.
Many protocol claims are better framed as “no materially profitable deviation.”

What to add:

An epsilon threshold at:
per-node level,
scenario level,
suite level.
Support absolute and relative regret thresholds.
Clear reporting of:
max regret,
mean regret,
regret distribution,
count of nodes exceeding epsilon.

This will make the checker much more operationally useful.

10. Better aggregation and diagnosis

You’ll want more than a flat regret table.

Why it matters:

Engineers need to know what failed and why.
Protocol designers need patterns across many runs.

What to add:

Aggregation by:
node type,
agent type,
scenario family,
protocol phase,
escalation tier.
Failure clustering:
repeated profitable late-challenge deviations,
repeated resolver-profit verdict distortions,
repeated timing-window exploits.
“Top 10 most severe deviations” summary.

This makes the checker actionable rather than merely analytical.

11. Counterfactual caching / memoization

This is less theoretically glamorous, but extremely high leverage.

Why it matters:

Replay-based subgame checking can get expensive quickly.
Deterministic environments often revisit identical or near-identical pre-states.

What to add:

Cache keyed by:
node ID,
pre-state hash,
alternative action,
continuation-policy version,
utility-function version.
Reuse of replay fragments where valid.

This will matter a lot once you move from isolated traces to suites.

12. First-class support for exogenous events and timing windows

In your domain, timing is often strategic, not incidental.

Why it matters:

Challenge timing, escalation deadlines, bond withdrawal windows, and same-block order can all change incentives.
A node’s profitability may depend on whether time advances, whether others can act first, or whether deadlines expire.

What to add:

Explicit modeling of:
wait/do nothing as an action,
deadline-sensitive alternatives,
same-block vs next-block alternatives,
ordering variants where relevant.
Clear treatment of time advancement as part of counterfactual enumeration.

This is very important for dispute-resolution realism.

Improvements that become especially important later
13. Sequential best-response refinement

After local regret checking, the next theoretical step is not just “did any deviation help?” but “is the continuation itself sequentially stable?”

That suggests:

iterating best responses,
refining continuation policies,
checking whether a candidate policy profile stabilizes.

This is more advanced, but it moves the system closer to a real equilibrium-finding or equilibrium-validation tool.

14. Multi-epoch / reputation-aware utility integration

If decisions affect future eligibility, reputation, resolver selection, or slashing exposure, single-trace utility may miss the real incentive.

High-value addition:

allow utility to include continuation value beyond the trace,
or plug into multi-epoch estimated continuation payoff.

This is especially important for resolver behavior.

15. Adversarial falsification mode

Beyond checking a declared trace, you may want the subgame engine to actively search for equilibrium-breaking deviations.

That means:

use the node library as a search space,
rank deviations by regret,
auto-surface counterexamples,
generate minimized failing traces.

This turns the checker into a protocol attack discovery tool, not just a validator.

My priority ranking

If I were prioritizing after Phase 4, I’d rank them roughly like this:

Top priority / essential
Continuation-policy semantics
Utility semantics
Information-set correctness
Replay-boundary semantics
Explicit inconclusive/inapplicable classifications
Next highest value
Alternative-generation quality
Counterfactual response modeling
Epsilon-equilibrium support
Multi-step deviation checking
Aggregation/diagnostic reporting
Strong later additions
Caching/memoization
Timing/exogenous event support
Sequential best-response refinement
Multi-epoch continuation value
Adversarial falsification mode
Bottom line

The biggest conceptual risk is not lack of counterfactuals anymore; it is ambiguous semantics around:

what counts as an available deviation,
what information the agent had,
how utility is computed,
and what future policy is assumed after deviation.

If those are made explicit, Phase 4 becomes a solid foundation. After that, the highest-value upgrade is expanding from one-step local regret to richer deviation search: better alternatives, better response modeling, and bounded multi-step deviations.

## Implementation-aligned vocabulary and key names (current)

To keep this document aligned with actual output, use the following canonical
field names and result vocabulary from the current evaluator/reporting path.

### Top-level evaluator result (counterfactual engine)

- Core status:
  - `:status` (`:pass | :fail | :inconclusive`)
  - `:spe-result` (`:spe/pass`, `:spe/epsilon-pass`, `:spe/fail-profitable-deviation`, etc.)
- Regret/epsilon:
  - `:max-regret`, `:mean-regret`, `:threshold`
  - `:epsilon-abs`, `:epsilon-rel`
  - `:exceed-epsilon-count`
- Coverage/classification:
  - `:checked-nodes`
  - `:proper-subgames-checked`
  - `:information-set-nodes-checked`
  - `:not-checkable-nodes`
  - `:class-counts`
- Semantics contracts:
  - `:continuation-policy`
  - `:replay-boundary`
  - `:utility-spec`
  - `:strategy-profile`
- Diagnostics:
  - `:memoization` (`{:enabled .. :entries .. :hits ..}`)
  - `:regret-distribution`
  - `:counterexamples`
  - `:off-path-coverage`

### Row-level regret-table fields

- Node identity/context:
  - `:node-index`, `:deterministic-key`, `:agent`, `:address`, `:node-type`
  - `:information-set`
- SPE checkability:
  - `:checkability`
  - `:spe/checkability`
  - `:checkability-reason`
- Action/utility:
  - `:chosen-action`, `:alternatives`
  - `:chosen-utility`, `:best-alt-utility`
  - `:local-regret`, `:bundle-regret`
- Policy binding:
  - `:governing-policy`

### Equilibrium-layer observed payload (`scenario/equilibrium.clj`)

The SPE concept payload uses `:observed` keys prefixed with `:spe-...`, including:

- `:spe-status`, `:spe-result`, `:spe-summary`
- `:spe-regret-table`, `:spe-max-regret`, `:spe-mean-regret`
- `:spe-threshold`, `:spe-epsilon-abs`, `:spe-epsilon-rel`
- `:spe-max-deviation-depth`
- `:spe-continuation-policy`, `:spe-replay-boundary`, `:spe-utility-spec`
- `:spe-strategy-profile`
- `:spe-proper-subgames-checked`, `:spe-information-set-nodes-checked`, `:spe-not-checkable-nodes`
- `:spe-class-counts`, `:spe-exceed-epsilon-count`
- `:spe-memoization`, `:spe-regret-distribution`
- `:spe-counterexamples`, `:spe-off-path-coverage`
- `:spe-proof-sketch`

### Proof-sketch alignment

When describing the proof sketch, prefer this structure:

1. **Claim** (bounded SPE proxy + strategy profile id)
2. **Method** (continuation-policy, utility-spec, max deviation depth, epsilon)
3. **Checked** (proper/info-set/not-checkable counts + memoization diagnostics)
4. **Result** (pass/fail/inconclusive summary, max regret, `:spe-result`, counterexample count on fail)

This keeps docs and emitted evidence synchronized and avoids terminology drift.
